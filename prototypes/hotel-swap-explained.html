The summary in one screen

Today's "Change Hotel" button is loud, repeated on every card, and competes with "Plan My Trip". Here's the proposal at a glance, then we'll walk through why.

What changes

  • Replace the pink button with a small grey "Change" pill on the top-right of each hotel card.
  • Tapping it opens a popup that slides up from the bottom (like Apple Maps or Uber).
  • The popup shows the current hotel pinned on top, then 3 alternatives we hand-picked, then a "See all hotels" link.
  • Each alternative shows the exact price change ("+₹4,500 / person") so there's no surprise.
  • Picking one swaps instantly. A small toast at the bottom says "Swapped to X · Undo" for 5 seconds.
  • We do not add a "are you sure?" popup. It would slow down the 60% of users who are just looking, not actually changing.

The situation

Why we're changing this

The user is on the itinerary page, just before paying. This is the highest drop-off moment in any travel app. Industry data (Booking, Expedia, Phocuswright) puts the drop between "view itinerary" and "checkout" at 40-60%.

People drop off because something feels off and they don't know how to fix it. The hotel swap feature exists to discharge doubt so the user feels in control and ready to pay.

The key insight. Most people who tap "Change Hotel" don't actually change anything. They just want to look and reassure themselves the recommendation is good. The job of this UX is to make that "looking" feel cheap and quick.
User psychology

What's actually going through their head

Three feelings at once:

  • Excitement. "This is real, I can see my trip." We protect this by not adding pop-ups or scary warnings.
  • Doubt. "Is this hotel actually good? Should I check?" We address this by letting them see alternatives without commitment.
  • Fear of missing out. "If I commit and there was a better deal, I lose." We address this by always showing the price difference upfront so there are no surprises.

What they want to do, ranked by how often:

  1. 60-70% · Just look at alternatives, then keep the original.
  2. 15-20% · Upgrade to something nicer (the "Upgrade to 5★" strip).
  3. 5-10% · Downgrade to save money.
  4. 5-10% · Switch to a different area of the city.

If the looker (60-70%) gets a clean, quick way to peek, they leave reassured. If they have to fight the UI, they leave anxious and abandon.

How the 3 picks adapt

What alternatives we show, by starting hotel tier

The 3 alternatives aren't a fixed list. They flex based on what the user already has. If they're starting at a 3-star, we show upgrades. If they're already at a 5-star, an "upgrade" pick makes no sense - we show side-grades instead.

The rule. The 3 picks must each open a different door. Same tier = waste. Always offer (a) something nicer, (b) something different in character, (c) something cheaper - filtered by what's actually possible from the user's current state.

Case D · Beach destination (Seminyak / Sanur)

Here we layer in destination-specific picks. For Ubud the categories are jungle / temple / rice fields. For Seminyak they are beachfront / poolside / nightlife.

  • Always include a "Beachfront" pick if the user is not currently on one - the most-asked-for swap on coastal stays.
  • Always include a "Adults only" or "Quieter" pick for couples (we know they're couples from the funnel).
  • Always include a stretch upgrade unless they're already maxed out.
Edge case: nothing valid to show. Rare, but possible (e.g. user is on the only 5★ in a small town). Then the sheet shows the original pinned + a single line: "We couldn't find better matches for these dates. Try changing days from the itinerary header." No empty list. No filler picks.
Question 3

3Should the swap happen on the same screen or a new page?

Three options for where the swap UI lives:

  • Inline expand on the card. The card itself grows downward to show alternatives. Looks janky on small phones.
  • Push a brand new full-screen page. User loses their place in the itinerary. Going back is friction.
  • Popup that slides up from the bottom (called a "bottom sheet"). The itinerary stays visible behind it. Dragging up makes it full screen if the user wants to dig deep.

This is the pattern Apple Maps, Uber, and Airbnb all use. The user keeps their context, and one closing gesture sends them back to exactly where they were.

Question 4

4Should the price update live as the user picks?

Already decided: yes, live. But where and how matters.

  • Show the price difference on each alternative ("+₹1,200 / person") before the user picks. No surprises after they Select.
  • The total in the footer updates with a small animation when they pick. So they see the change land.
  • Cheaper options show "-₹2,000" in green. But don't celebrate it - users get suspicious of dramatic discounts.
  • Don't show a struck-through original price. It feels like discount/sales language and isn't what we are.

Why this works

Price uncertainty is the single biggest source of cart abandonment in travel apps. Showing the delta on every option turns the price from a hidden threat into a clear trade-off the user can reason about.

Showing it on the footer too means even if the user goes back to the itinerary, they remember the total updated.

Question 5

5Should "Change" and "Upgrade" be the same thing?

Today the card has both: a pink "Change Hotel" button and an "Upgrade to 5★" strip. Are these one thing or two?

My answer: two different things. Keep them separate.

Reason:

  • "Change" is sideways. Try a similar hotel. Low commitment, no extra money usually. The 60% just-looking crowd lives here.
  • "Upgrade" is selling. Adds money. The 15% who want nicer rooms live here. This is where we make margin.

If we mix them, every "just looking" tap becomes a sales conversation, which scares off the casual user. Keep them as two separate paths.

Question 6

6Confirm before swap, or instant + undo?

  • "Are you sure?" pop-up after every swap. Slows everyone. Trains users to dismiss dialogs without reading.
  • Instant swap + a "Swapped · Undo" toast for 5 seconds. This is the Gmail pattern. Fast, forgiving.

Why this is safe: swapping is reversible. You can swap again. There's nothing to "confirm" because nothing bad happens.

Question 7

7Two doors: "see this hotel" vs "swap this hotel"

A subtle but important split. Each hotel card should have two different actions with two different meanings:

  • Tap the hotel image, title, or body → go to the hotel's detail page. This is "I want to learn more about this hotel".
  • Tap the small "Change" pill → open the swap popup. This is "I want to consider alternatives".

Mixing these two (e.g. "tap Change → see hotel details") is the most common mistake in travel apps. Expedia historically conflated them and saw measured drops in actual swap rates.

If they tap an alternative inside the swap popup, we open that hotel's detail page on top of the popup. Closing it brings them back to the popup with their place saved. Two doors, both clearly labeled.

Recommendation

Putting it all together

  1. Replace the loud pink "Change Hotel" button with a small grey "Change" pill, top-right of each card.
  2. Tap = popup slides up from bottom. Current hotel pinned, then 3 hand-picked alternatives with reason badges.
  3. Each alternative shows price change ("+₹1,200" / "Same price" / "-₹500").
  4. A "See all Ubud hotels" link at the bottom expands the popup to full screen.
  5. Picking is instant. A "Swapped · Undo" toast appears for 5 seconds.
  6. Footer price updates with a small animation.
  7. "Upgrade to 5★" stays a separate strip with its own price label.
